Module 01

Prompt Engineering — The Art of Asking

🧞 The Genie Analogy

Imagine a genie that does exactly what you say — not what you mean. You ask: "Make me a sandwich." It places one on the ceiling. Technically correct. Totally useless. Prompt Engineering is learning to speak genie — crafting your request so precisely that you always get what you actually want.

AI models are powerful — but also literal. The same question, asked two different ways, can produce a brilliant answer or a useless one. Prompt Engineering is the skill of talking to AI effectively. No coding required. Anyone can learn it today.

Trick 1 — Give AI a Role
Instead of asking "explain investing", try: "You are a patient financial advisor speaking to a nervous first-time investor." You gave the AI a costume — it behaves completely differently. More careful, simpler vocabulary, more reassuring.
Trick 2 — Ask It To Think Step By Step
Adding "think step by step" to hard questions makes AI dramatically more accurate. It forces the model to slow down and show its work — exactly like your maths teacher insisted. Fewer shortcuts = fewer mistakes.
Trick 3 — Show It an Example First
Show the AI what a great answer looks like before asking your question. It will pattern-match your example. This is called "few-shot prompting" — one of the highest-leverage techniques available, used by professionals every day.
📊 Real Business Case
Why Senior Consultants Get 10× More From AI Than Juniors

Management consultants use AI to draft client reports, analyse data, and build presentations — tasks that used to take days.

The difference? Senior consultants write prompts that specify the audience, tone, output format, length, and constraints — all at once. The AI produces near-final work. Juniors write vague prompts, get generic output, then spend hours editing.

✦ Outcome: Same AI. Same model. The quality of the prompt determines whether you save 2 hours or 2 days. Prompt Engineering is already a competitive career skill — and it costs nothing to learn.

Module 02

RAG — The Open-Book Test

🎒 Two Students in an Exam

Student A relies purely on memory — everything learned before walking in. Student B gets a backpack full of books and can look things up before writing each answer. Who gives more accurate, current answers? Student B — every time. RAG gives AI a backpack.

Standard AI learned everything during training — then stopped learning. Ask about last week's news or your company's internal files and it either guesses or admits ignorance.

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) changes this. Before answering, the AI searches a connected library — your company files, a product catalogue, medical journals — and uses that fresh, specific information to build its answer.

How Does It Find the Right Page in Millions of Documents?
Not by keyword-matching like Google. Every document is converted into numbers capturing its meaning. Your question also becomes numbers. The system finds documents whose meaning is mathematically closest to your question — "dog" and "puppy" land near each other; "dog" and "tax returns" land in different countries on this map of meaning.
⚠️ Where RAG Fails — Hallucination
If the answer isn't in the library, the AI sometimes invents one — confidently. This is hallucination, like Student B finding nothing in their backpack but writing something anyway. Good RAG systems are designed to say "I couldn't find a reliable source" instead of guessing.
📊 Real Business Case
How Klarna Handles Millions of Customer Queries with AI

Klarna needed a support AI that could answer questions about real-time orders, policies, and account details — information that changes daily.

They built with RAG: before every response, the AI searches Klarna's live policy documents, order database, and FAQ library. It doesn't memorise policies — it looks them up fresh, in real time, for every customer.

✦ Outcome: The AI handles the equivalent of hundreds of full-time support agents. When policies update, the team updates one document — the AI is automatically current. No retraining needed.

Module 03

Fine-Tuning — Specialty School

⛸️ The Athlete Goes to Skating School

Jordan is a solid all-rounder — can swim, run, play basketball, do gymnastics. Then Jordan trains intensively for 6 months in figure skating. Afterwards, Jordan can still do everything before — but is now exceptional at skating specifically. Fine-Tuning is sending a general AI to specialty school.

A general AI is like a well-read generalist — knows a bit about everything. Fine-Tuning immerses that generalist in thousands of examples of one specific thing: legal contracts, medical diagnoses, customer service scripts, cooking recipes.

The result isn't just an AI with more facts. It develops a new way of thinking and communicating — absorbing the tone, vocabulary, and priorities of its specialty. A fine-tuned Nike customer service AI doesn't just know the return policy — it sounds like Nike. That's identity, not just information.

⚠️ The Hidden Danger — Bias Baked In
A famous case: a hiring AI was fine-tuned on a company's past hiring decisions. Most past hires were men. The AI silently learned to downrank women's resumes. Nobody programmed this. It absorbed the bias from the data. Fine-Tuning learns everything in your examples — including the mistakes and prejudices.
RAG vs Fine-Tuning — The Critical Difference
RAG changes what the AI knows — update a document, AI is current immediately.

Fine-Tuning changes how the AI thinks — its reasoning style, personality, vocabulary. Can't be updated by swapping a file. Requires full retraining — which costs significant time and money.
📊 Real Business Case
How Bloomberg Built an AI That Thinks Like a Financial Analyst

Bloomberg trained a general AI on 40 years of financial news, analyst reports, earnings calls, and market data. The result — BloombergGPT — doesn't just know about finance. It reasons like a financial professional.

A general AI might say a stock "went up." BloombergGPT says it experienced "a bullish breakout supported by stronger-than-expected EBITDA margin expansion." Same event — completely different depth and usefulness.

✦ Outcome: Fine-Tuning turned a general model into a genuine specialist. Bloomberg's clients get analysis that reads like a senior analyst wrote it — not a generic chatbot.

🧩 Combined Real-World Example
A Legal AI Assistant at a Major Law Firm

Fine-Tuning layer: Trained on thousands of legal cases and contracts. Thinks like a lawyer — uses legal language naturally, understands precedent, reasons about risk the way a trained professional would.

RAG layer: When you ask about a specific client's matter, the AI retrieves that client's actual contracts and emails in real time. It didn't memorise these — it looks them up fresh for every query.

Prompt Engineering layer: Lawyers have crafted tested prompts: "Identify liability risks and explain them to a non-lawyer client in plain English, under 200 words, no hedging." This shapes output format, audience, and tone every time.

✦ Remove any one layer and the tool degrades noticeably. Fine-Tuning provides the expertise. RAG provides the facts. Prompt Engineering provides the shape. All three together is what separates a professional tool from a toy.

Module 05

Trade-offs & Real Challenges

Every tool in this course is powerful — and every tool has a way it quietly fails. The difference between a good AI implementation and a bad one isn't which tool you picked. It's whether you understood where each one starts to break.

🗣️ Prompt Engineering — The Brittleness Problem

Imagine a company with 500 different AI-powered features — each controlled by a carefully written prompt. Their AI provider quietly ships a model update. Overnight, all 500 prompts behave slightly differently. Nobody changed a line of code. The prompts just interact with the new model differently.

This is called prompt brittleness. Prompts aren't like code — they don't version-control cleanly, they have no unit tests, and a single word change can flip the output. At scale, this becomes a maintenance nightmare nobody anticipated at launch.

Works Well When
• You have a small number of prompts
• Task is well-defined and consistent
• Speed to deploy matters most
Breaks Down When
• AI model gets updated silently
• You need consistent results at scale
• High-stakes decisions rely on output
📚 RAG — The Garbage Document Problem

RAG is only as good as the documents in its library. The AI doesn't fact-check what it retrieves — it trusts whatever lands in front of it. One outdated policy file, one internally contradictory document, one subtly wrong number — and the AI builds a confident answer around it.

There's also the context window problem: the AI can only hold a limited slice of retrieved documents in its working memory at once. If the answer requires understanding five documents together but only two fit in memory, it might miss the crucial connection — like being handed three torn pages from three different books and asked to understand the full plot.

Works Well When
• Documents are well-maintained
• Questions are focused and specific
• Info changes frequently
Breaks Down When
• Document library is stale or messy
• Answer spans many documents at once
• No governance process for documents
⚙️ Fine-Tuning — The Alignment Tax & Catastrophic Forgetting

When you Fine-Tune a general AI, it gets better at the specific task — and can quietly get worse at everything else. Train it too hard on one domain and it may start losing general reasoning ability. This is called catastrophic forgetting — specialist training overwrites some of the general knowledge.

There's a deeper problem: general AI models are trained to be safe, balanced, and careful. Fine-Tuning erodes this. A sales AI trained to be persuasive might become subtly manipulative. A news AI trained to maximise engagement might start favouring drama over accuracy. Researchers call this the alignment tax — making AI more useful for a narrow purpose often costs you some of its broader trustworthiness. Nobody programs this in. It just happens.

Works Well When
• Domain is stable and well-defined
• High-quality, balanced training data
• Budget for evaluation & monitoring
Breaks Down When
• Training data is biased or one-sided
• Domain changes rapidly after training
• Safety properties aren't re-evaluated
The Pattern Underneath All Three
Every tool that makes AI more capable introduces a new way it can fail quietly. Prompts break silently when models update. RAG produces confident wrong answers when documents are bad. Fine-Tuning absorbs bias and erodes safety without anyone noticing. The best AI builders aren't just the ones who can build these systems — they're the ones who anticipate and catch these failures before they cause harm.
⚠️ Cautionary Real-World Case
When It All Goes Wrong — The Airline Chatbot

In 2023, a major airline's customer service chatbot told a grieving customer that bereavement fares could be booked retroactively after the fact. The customer relied on this advice, booked at full price, and asked for a refund.

The airline argued the chatbot's answer was not binding. A Canadian court disagreed and ordered the airline to honour it. The AI had given a confident, plausible-sounding answer that directly contradicted actual policy — and the company was held legally responsible.

⚠ The lesson: No RAG to ground the answer in real policy, weak prompt guardrails, and no output validation created a legal liability. These aren't just engineering problems. Each failure mode from this module played a role.

Module 06

The Decision Framework

You now understand all three tools and their failure modes. The last skill is knowing which one to reach for — and when. Work through each scenario below.

Scenario A — A startup wants to add AI to their customer support. Their product updates monthly. Budget is tight. What should they start with?
📚RAG + Prompt Engineering is the right answer. Fine-Tuning is expensive to retrain when products change monthly. RAG means they just update help docs — the AI is instantly current. A well-written prompt controls tone and safety. This is the lean, maintainable architecture for an early-stage team.
✓ Correct thinking. RAG + Prompt Engineering is the lean, maintainable stack for a fast-moving team. They update help docs when products change — no retraining needed. Prompt Engineering wraps everything to control tone and safety. Fine-Tuning comes later, when they have scale and stable data.
🗣️Prompt Engineering alone will hit a ceiling. It's a great starting point — and they should definitely invest in it — but without RAG, the AI has no access to their actual product information. It will either make things up or give generic answers. Add RAG as soon as possible.
Scenario B — A hospital wants an AI that speaks, reasons, and prioritises like their senior doctors — and stays current with monthly clinical guideline updates. What's the right architecture?
⚙️Fine-Tuning alone leaves a dangerous gap. It gives the AI clinical reasoning depth from 20 years of notes — but monthly guideline updates will make it stale within weeks. Add RAG for the guidelines database, and Prompt Engineering to ensure outputs are structured and safe.
📚RAG alone misses the clinical depth. RAG keeps the AI current with guidelines — but without Fine-Tuning, it reasons like a generalist, not a doctor. The vocabulary, the prioritisation, the diagnostic logic — those come from training on real clinical data. You need both.
✓ Perfect architecture. All three layers for good reason. Fine-Tuning instils clinical reasoning from 20 years of real cases. RAG keeps monthly guidelines current with zero retraining. Prompt Engineering ensures outputs are formatted for doctors and include appropriate safety caveats. In healthcare, skipping any layer is a patient safety risk.
Scenario C — Your AI starts giving outdated pricing info six months after launch. Nobody changed the model. Products changed. What do you fix, and how?
⚙️Retraining works — but it's the hardest fix. It takes weeks, costs real money, and you'll face this same problem every time pricing changes. The root issue is that Fine-Tuning was used for something that changes frequently. Switch to RAG for pricing data going forward.
✓ This is exactly right. Update one document. Done. If pricing lives in a RAG-connected document, updating it takes minutes and the AI is instantly current. This is why fast-changing information should always live in RAG, not baked into a Fine-Tuned model.
🗣️Prompt injection of pricing is a workaround, not a fix. It's fragile, manual, and won't scale. The right solution is to put pricing information in a RAG-connected document. Update the document once — the AI is current everywhere, instantly.
The Three Questions That Determine Your Architecture
Before picking a tool, ask these three questions in order:

1. Does the information change frequently? If yes → RAG. Never bake fast-moving data into Fine-Tuning.

2. Does the AI need to sound, reason, or behave like a specialist? If yes → Fine-Tuning. Prompts can't teach expertise.

3. Do you control the output format, tone, and safety? Always → Prompt Engineering. It's the layer that never goes away.
Glossary

Every concept introduced in this course — with plain-English definitions. Filter by topic.

Prompt EngineeringPE
The practice of designing and refining the text you give an AI to produce better, more targeted outputs. No coding required — it's about learning to ask precisely.
Few-Shot PromptingPE
Giving the AI 2–5 examples of the output you want before asking your question. The AI pattern-matches your examples and produces results in the same style and format.
Chain-of-ThoughtPE
A prompting technique where you ask the AI to "think step by step" before giving an answer. Forces the model to reason through a problem rather than jumping to conclusions — dramatically improves accuracy on complex tasks.
Prompt BrittlenessPE
The tendency of prompts to behave differently when the underlying AI model is updated — even if the prompt itself hasn't changed. A major scaling problem: 400 prompts can break overnight from a single silent model update.
TemperaturePE
A setting that controls how creative vs. predictable the AI's output is. High temperature = more varied, more surprising, occasionally wrong. Low temperature = more consistent, safer, sometimes boring. Set by whoever deploys the AI.
RAGRAG
Retrieval-Augmented Generation. A technique where the AI searches a connected document library before answering a question, using fresh retrieved information to build its response rather than relying solely on training memory.
Vector EmbeddingsRAG
The way RAG finds relevant documents — by converting both documents and your question into lists of numbers that represent their meaning. Documents close in this "meaning space" get retrieved. This is why RAG finds conceptually related content, not just keyword matches.
Context WindowRAG
The maximum amount of text an AI can hold in its "working memory" at one time. RAG systems must choose which retrieved chunks fit within this limit — if the answer requires many documents at once, some may get cut off.
HallucinationRAG
When an AI produces a confident, detailed, plausible-sounding answer that is simply wrong — because the correct information was never in its training data or RAG library. The danger: it sounds just as authoritative as a correct answer.
Document GovernanceRAG
The practice of assigning ownership, expiry dates, and update responsibilities to every document in a RAG library. Without it, stale documents pile up and the AI gives confidently outdated answers — the garbage document problem.
Fine-TuningFT
The process of taking a pre-trained general AI model and training it further on a specialised dataset — so it learns a specific domain's vocabulary, reasoning style, and priorities. Changes how the AI thinks, not just what it knows.
Alignment TaxFT
The phenomenon where Fine-Tuning a model for a narrow task erodes its original safety and fairness guardrails. A sales AI becomes more persuasive — and also more willing to bend the truth. Nobody programs this; it's an emergent consequence of the training process.
Catastrophic ForgettingFT
When intensive Fine-Tuning on a specialist domain overwrites some of the model's general capabilities. The AI becomes a better skater — and forgets how to swim. Heavy Fine-Tuning requires careful evaluation to catch capability regressions.
Training Data BiasFT
When the dataset used to Fine-Tune an AI reflects historical human biases — skewed hiring decisions, unequal representation, past unfair practices. The AI learns these patterns faithfully and reproduces them at scale, silently and confidently.
Base ModelFT
The large, general-purpose AI that Fine-Tuning starts from. Think of it as the athlete before specialty school — capable at everything, expert at nothing. GPT-4, Claude, and Llama are examples of base models that get Fine-Tuned into specialists.
DriftRisk
The gradual degradation of AI output quality over time — as the world changes but the model or its documents don't. Pricing drift, guideline drift, tone drift. The output seems fine until someone notices it's been wrong for weeks.
